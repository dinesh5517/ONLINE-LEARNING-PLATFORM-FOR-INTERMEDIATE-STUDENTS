Classification of Elements & Periodicity - Short Notes

1. Historical Development

Dobereiner's Triads: Groups of 3 elements with similar properties where atomic weight of middle element was average of other two.

Newlands' Octaves: Every 8th element had properties similar to first when arranged by atomic weights.

Mendeleev's Periodic Table: Based on atomic weights with gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties.

Modern Periodic Law: Properties are periodic functions of their atomic numbers (Mosley, 1913).

4. Types of Elements

s-Block Elements
Groups 1-2 (alkali & alkaline earth metals), highly reactive, form ionic compounds
p-Block Elements
Groups 13-18 (metals, metalloids, non-metals, noble gases)
d-Block Elements
Transition metals (Groups 3-12), form colored compounds, show variable oxidation states
f-Block Elements
Lanthanides & Actinides (inner transition elements), radioactive elements

5. Anomalous Properties

Second Period Anomalies: Li, Be, B show diagonal relationships with Mg, Al, Si respectively due to similar charge/radius ratio.

Exceptions:

  • N has higher IE than O due to stable half-filled p orbital
  • Electron gain enthalpy of O or F is less negative than S or Cl
  • Cr and Cu have exceptional electronic configurations

6. Important Definitions

Ionization Energy:
Energy required to remove most loosely bound electron from isolated gaseous atom
Electron Affinity:
Energy released when electron is added to isolated gaseous atom
Electronegativity:
Tendency of atom to attract shared pair of electrons (Pauling scale)

7. Applications of Periodicity

  • Predicting chemical behavior of elements
  • Understanding chemical bonding and reactivity
  • Designing new materials with specific properties
  • Explaining acid-base character of oxides and hydroxides
  • Understanding biological importance of elements
