Chemical Kinetics - EAMCET Short Notes

1. Introduction to Chemical Kinetics

  • Chemical kinetics is the study of rates of chemical reactions and factors affecting these rates.
  • It investigates how quickly reactants are consumed and products are formed.
  • Key parameters include reaction rate, rate constant, reaction order, and activation energy.
  • Chemical kinetics helps understand reaction mechanisms and optimize reaction conditions.

3. Rate Law and Order of Reaction

  • Rate Law: An equation showing how reaction rate depends on concentrations of reactants.
  • For reaction aA + bB â†’ Products: Rate = k[A]x[B]y
  • Where k is the rate constant, x and y are the orders with respect to reactants A and B.
  • Overall Order = x + y
  • Types of Reactions Based on Order:
    • Zero Order: Rate = k; rate independent of concentration
    • First Order: Rate = k[A]; rate proportional to concentration
    • Second Order: Rate = k[A]2 or Rate = k[A][B]; rate proportional to square of concentration

4. Integrated Rate Laws

  • Integrated rate laws relate concentration directly to time.
  • Zero Order: [A]t = [A]0 - kt
  • First Order: ln[A]t = ln[A]0 - kt
  • Second Order: 1/[A]t = 1/[A]0 + kt
  • These laws help determine reaction order from experimental data.

5. Half-life of a Reaction

  • Definition: Time taken for the concentration of a reactant to reduce to half its initial value.
  • For first-order reactions, half-life is independent of initial concentration: t1/2 = 0.693/k
  • For zero-order reactions: t1/2 = [A]0/2k
  • For second-order reactions: t1/2 = 1/(k[A]0)

6. Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  • Nature of Reactants: Physical state, chemical nature, and bond strength affect reaction rates.
  • Concentration: Higher concentrations generally increase reaction rates.
  • Temperature: Most reaction rates increase with temperature (Arrhenius equation).
  • Presence of Catalyst: Catalysts increase reaction rates by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.
  • Surface Area: Increasing surface area of solid reactants increases reaction rates.
  • Pressure: For gaseous reactions, increasing pressure often increases reaction rates.

7. Arrhenius Equation

  • The Arrhenius equation relates the rate constant to temperature:
    • k = A e(-Ea/RT)
  • Where:
    • k = rate constant
    • A = frequency factor or pre-exponential factor
    • Ea = activation energy
    • R = gas constant (8.314 J/molÂ·K)
    • T = absolute temperature (K)
  • The logarithmic form is useful for determining activation energy:
    • ln k = ln A - Ea/RT
  • A plot of ln k vs. 1/T gives a straight line with slope = -Ea/R

8. Collision Theory and Reaction Mechanism

  • Collision Theory: For a reaction to occur, molecules must collide with:
    • Sufficient energy (greater than activation energy)
    • Proper orientation
  • Reaction Mechanism: The sequence of elementary steps by which a reaction occurs.
  • Elementary Step: A single-step reaction with no intermediates.
  • Rate-Determining Step: The slowest step in a reaction mechanism that controls the overall reaction rate.
  • Molecularity: The number of molecules or atoms coming together in an elementary step.

Tips for EAMCET

  • Understand the difference between order and molecularity of reactions.
  • Practice calculating reaction rates, rate constants, and half-lives.
  • Memorize the integrated rate equations and their graphical representations.
  • Learn to determine reaction order from experimental data.
  • Know how to apply the Arrhenius equation to calculate activation energy.
  • Focus on numerical problems related to first-order reactions, as they are most common in exams.
  • Understand how various factors affect reaction rates and be able to explain why.
  • Practice interpreting reaction mechanisms and identifying rate-determining steps.
